# Arabic Grammar Map — Conclusive Reference

The complete classification tree of Arabic **nahw** and **sarf**, as far down as the
grammar genuinely branches. This is the *reference territory* — the app
(`../README.md`) teaches a beginner subset of it.

---

## The organizing principle

A flat list of "the ways you can classify an ism" never feels complete, because
nothing tells you the list is closed. It is closed, and here is the spine that
closes it:

> **Every ism and every fiʿl is classified two ways — morphologically (ṣarf, what
> the word is by its form, in isolation) and syntactically (naḥw, the role it plays
> in a sentence). There is no third way.** Harf has no morphology to speak of; it is
> classified by meaning and by what it governs.

- **ṣarf** = the word before it enters a sentence — its number, pattern, root,
  soundness, ending.
- **naḥw** = the word inside a sentence — its iʿrāb (case/mood) and the operators
  that assign it.

So the map below is exhaustive not by accumulation but by construction: it walks
each word-type down its ṣarf axes, then its naḥw axis, and stops where the two
sciences stop.

## Notes on the non-obvious choices

- **maʿrifah = 7, including munādā.** The strict classical count is 6 (ḍamir, ʿalam,
  ishārah, mawṣul, bi-al, muḍāf); munādā is added as a 7th — definite by the intent of
  the call (al-maqṣud bi-l-nidāʾ). The 7-count matches the Indo-Pak / Bengali madrasa
  tradition the app follows.
- **Signs of iʿrāb are orthogonal to the states.** aṣli / farʿi / taqdiri describe *how*
  a case shows, not *which* case — every state can appear via any sign. They are a
  separate branch, not siblings of marfuʿ / manṣub / majrur.
- **Tawābiʿ belong to all three states.** naʿt, taʾkid, badal, and ʿaṭf inherit the
  case of whatever they follow, so they sit above the three-way split, not inside one
  branch of it.
- **The nawāsikh are naḥw operators, not a sarf class of verb.** kāna, inna, kāda,
  ẓanna and friends are grouped under fiʿl-naḥw because their job is to rewrite the
  iʿrāb of the nominal sentence — which is exactly why their outputs (ism kāna,
  khabar inna, …) reappear under the marfuʿ and manṣub branches above.

## Relation to the app

The app does not march a beginner through this whole tree. It teaches the
recognition-level core — the top axes of each word-type, the iʿrāb states, and the main
sub-classes (mushtaqq, muʿtall, asbāb al-manʿ, the nawāsikh, the ḥarf groups) — and
leaves the deepest tail (full ṣarf/awzān conjugation tables, jamʿ-taksir patterns, the
signs-of-iʿrāb detail, afʿāl al-taʿajjub, …) as reference. See `../README.md` for exactly
what is built and the nested-page recipe for extending into any node not yet built.
